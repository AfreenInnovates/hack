It was July 20, 1969, and the world was holding its breath as NASA's Apollo 11 mission made history by landing on the moon. Neil Armstrong, the mission commander, stepped out of the lunar module Eagle and onto the moon's surface, famously declaring, "That's one small step for man, one giant leap for mankind."

But just behind Armstrong, waiting to make his own mark on the moon's surface, was Edwin "Buzz" Aldrin, the mission's lunar module pilot. As the second person to walk on the moon, Aldrin had a unique perspective on the incredible achievement.

Born on January 20, 1930, in Montclair, New Jersey, Aldrin grew up with a passion for science and mathematics. He earned a degree in mechanical engineering from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) and later received a doctorate in astronautics from MIT.
